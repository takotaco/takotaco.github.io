Constraints and Inspiration

The cardboard bench was a project for the "Industrial Design Fundamentals" class I took in the Spring of 2013 at Carnegie Mellon.

The requirements for the project were simple: make a chair; make it entirely out of cardboard.

First I gathered inspiration, some of which you can see here. I wanted to make a chair that was as creepy as possible: something that you wouldn't want to sit in even if you could.

Initial Sketches

Next I did some research. What did chairs look like? What forms were possible? To answer this I went to the Carnegie Art Museum to sketch some of the chairs in their collection.

After my research, I did some sketches of what my chair might look like. How could I make something out of cardboard that would support a human and also look like a centipede?

Prototype

After I came up with an idea that I liked, I made a scaled-down prototype. Since it was so small, I used card stock to approximate cardboard.

I tested the prototype for weight capacity and ease of assembly. Since I wasn't allowed to use any material other than cardboard, I had to design the bench in such a way that it could be put together without glue, tape, or staples. I also had to make sure it wouldn't fall apart as soon as someone sat on it.

The Final Result

After finding success with my prototype, I made a full scale version! I measured and cut this entirely by hand out of 2-ply cardboard.

It can fit and support about three people.

Ideas & Prototype

Project Flow was a project I worked on for a class called "Tech Startup Lab" in the Fall of 2012 at Carnegie Mellon. To get into the class students had to form a team and pitch an idea for a startup to the professor. My idea was to create a "how-to" website that used flowcharts rather than lists or photos to convey instructions.

I lead the project and worked with two other people: one business person and one other developer. The code in this prototype is entirely my own.

When designing the interface I wanted to eliminate the need to select different tools from a menu to create and move the boxes and arrows. I wanted to make the interface as intuitive as possible so that users could make flowcharts quickly. I also wanted to use the HTML5 canvas as it had recently been released and I thought — and still think — that it's awesome.

Sketch

This was a personal project that I worked on from May through December 2014.

First I drew a sketch of the image I had in my mind. I made the image very complex to take advantage of the fact that a laser would be cutting it out for me.

Vector Outline

After I finalized the sketch I made a vector drawing. I did this to take advantage of the fact that a laser will cut faster and more accurately given a vector image rather than a raster image.

I colorized the lines to indicate what the laser should do: I wanted some parts of the material to be engraved at different depths and some parts to be cut all the way through.

The Final Result

As the final step I took my designs down to my local laser cutting facility and got it cut out of plywood. I was quite pleased with the result!
