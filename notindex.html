Constraints and Inspiration

The cardboard bench was a project for an Industrial Design for non-majors class I took in the spring of 2013.

The requirements for the project were simple: make a chair; make it entirely out of cardboard and nothing else.

To the right you can see some of my inspiration. Initially I wanted to make a chair as creepy as possible: something that you would't want to sit in even if you could.

Initial Sketches

Next I did some research. What did chairs look like? What forms were possible? Here you can see some of the sketches I did while examining a wall full of chairs at the Carnegie Art Museum.

After researching chairs in general I did some sketches of what my chair might look like. How could I make something out of cardboard that would support a human and also look like a centipede? A bench seemed appropriate.

I did a lot more sketches that I don't have room for here.

Prototype

After I came up with an idea I liked, I made a scaled-down prototype. Since it was so small I used card stock to approximate cardboard.

I tested the prototype for weight capacity and ease of assembly. Since I wasn't allowed to use any material other than cardboard, I had to design the bench in such a way that it would hold together without glue, tape, or staples. I also had to make sure it wouldn't fall apart as soon as someone sat on it.

The Final Result

After finding success with my prototype, I made a full scale version! I measured and cut this entirely by hand out of 2-ply cardboard. It can fit and support about three people.

Ideas & Prototype

Project Flow was a project I worked on for a class called "Tech Startup Lab" in the Fall of 2012. My idea was to create a "how-to" website that used flowcharts rather than lists or photos to convey instructions. The vision was that Project Flow could be used to describe more complex processes than the average "how-to" site and thus become insanely popular.

Safe to say that never happened, but you can still try out the prototype!

I lead the project and worked with two other people, one business person and one other developer. The code in this prototype is entirely my own.

The main goal I had when designing the interface was to eliminate the need to select different tools from a menu to create the boxes and arrows or to move them around. I wanted to make the interface as intuitive as possible so that users could make flowcharts quickly. I also wanted to use the HTML5 canvas as it had recently been released and I thought (and still think) it's awesome.

If I were to ever go back and continue working on this project, the next step would be to do user testing.

Sketch

I worked on this project on and off from May through December 2014.

First I made a sketch of the image I had in my mind. Initially I wanted to make the image as complex a possible to take advantage of the fact that a laser would be cutting it out for me.

To get the bee just right I did some research on bee anatomy. Using reference images I was able to make sure the image in my head came out on paper as an accurate representation of a bee.

I used Adobe Photoshop and my Wacom tablet to create this sketch.

Vector Outline

After I finalized the sketch I went back over the outline with a vector tool in Adobe Illustrator. I did this for two reasons: first, I found out that a laser will cut faster and more accurately given a vector image rather than a raster image; second, I wanted the practice. This step took a long time. At this point I decided to exclude the flower from the final design for time reasons.

As part of this step I also spent time colorizing the vector lines based on what I wanted the laser to do. Here I wanted the laser to do two depths or darknesses of engraving and one pass of cutting all the way through the material.

Note that the bee also only has four legs! I chose to omit the other two because I felt that that area became too busy and hard to understand with all six legs.

The Final Result

As the final step I took my designs down to my local laser cutting facility and got it cut out of 3-ply plywood. I was quite pleased with the result!

If I were to do this project again, I'd omit the engraving lines where they overlapped with the cutting lines. This made the really thin areas, like the wings, extremely fragile.
